Dormant Digital Collections, AI Triage

Lessons from the Islam West Africa Collection and beyond

Frédérick Madore Data Curator Africa Multiple Cluster of Excellence
Scan for these slidesslides.frederickmadore.com
I'll open on the hook, not the title. Arlette Farge wrote about the allure of the archive: the slow, physical encounter with a document. Most of us now work very differently. We come back from fieldwork with thousands of scans we will never read. My argument today is simple: AI won't replace close reading, but it can triage — it can tell us where close reading is worth our time. The examples come from a collection on Islam in West Africa, but the lessons travel to any region's archives, including Asia.

Use 1 · Text extraction

Multimodal LLMs read the page like a person

Layout recognition failure: traditional OCR scrambles a multi-column page.

  • Traditional OCR breaks on multi-column papers, tables, handwriting, quick phone scans
  • Even a 5–10% error rate matters: one typo in a name hides a document
  • "Ouédraogo" → "Oueclaoqo": the source is there, but no one will find it
  • Multimodal AI handles it in one pass: printed & handwritten, French & Arabic, messy layout
Strongest so far Google Gemini 3 Pro — the best model I have tested for this.
Start with text extraction. Traditional OCR was built for clean, single-column print. It breaks on West African newspapers: multiple columns, tables, handwriting, a quick phone scan. And a five-to-ten percent error rate isn't a rounding error. One typo in a name buries the document: "Ouédraogo" comes out as nonsense, and now no one searching that very common name will ever find it. Multimodal models read the page the way a person does: layout, printed and handwritten, French and Arabic, in a single pass. Gemini 3 Pro is the strongest I've tested so far.

Highlighted: the writer's own spelling slips, kept verbatim — the model didn't silently correct them.

Harder still: cursive. This is a handwritten letter from 1991 — Muslim associations in Burkina Faso writing to a minister to protest unequal media treatment of Muslim and Christian holidays. Traditional OCR does nothing with this; the model transcribed the whole letter. And look at what it kept — the writer's own spelling slips, highlighted: "Sunite" for Sunnite, "facheuse", "chretien", and a "Decembre" that flatly contradicts the correctly-accented "décembre" two lines above. It preserved them rather than tidying them away — exactly the fidelity a historian wants, because the errors are part of the record. Though, as the very next slide warns, it does not always behave so well.

Use 2 · Transcription

Audio and video transcription

  • The same multimodal logic extends to audio and video
  • Transcripts return with timestamps and speaker labels
  • IWAC corpus: ~155 hours of Nigerian preachers, mostly Hausa & Arabic
  • Early tests in Hausa, Arabic, Kurmanji Kurdish — promising as a first draft
The same logic extends to sound and video. You get a transcript back with timestamps and speaker labels. The IWAC has about a hundred and fifty-five hours of Nigerian preachers, mostly in Hausa and Arabic — material I could never transcribe by hand. Early tests in Hausa, Arabic, even Kurmanji Kurdish are promising as a first draft. Not publishable as it stands, but enough to find the ten minutes worth a full listen.

A caution

Cleaner than the source

  • AI-OCR and audio-to-text open previously unusable material
  • The model silently modernises spellings, standardises toponyms, smooths regional variants
  • It can also hallucinate: invent text that was never there
  • The failure mode is harder to spot: a fluent paragraph with invented detail
The discipline Keep the original image alongside the text. Verify against the scan before you quote.
Now the danger, and it's the most important slide. These tools open material that was effectively unusable. But they don't just transcribe — they tidy. The model silently modernises spellings, standardises place-names, smooths regional variants. And it can hallucinate: invent text that was never on the page. The failure mode is the problem. Not garble you would catch, but a fluent, plausible paragraph with invented detail. So the discipline is non-negotiable: keep the original image beside the text, and verify against the scan before you quote a single word.

Use 3 · Extraction at scale

Entities and topics: who, where, what, about what

Entities linked across the corpus.

  • Manual keyword tagging: unworkable across thousands of documents
  • Standard DH tools trained on Western text — they miss local names
  • OCR noise compounds the problem
  • LLMs extract not just entities but topics, given the right prompt
A feasible query, now "Find me every document that discusses secularism or the pilgrimage."
Third use: entities and topics. Tagging thousands of documents by hand is hopeless. The standard digital-humanities tools were trained on Western text and miss local names, and OCR noise makes it worse. Language models, given careful instructions, pull out not just names and places but topics — what a document is actually about. So "find me everything that touches secularism, or the pilgrimage" becomes a query you can really run across the whole collection.

Once you've extracted all that, the pile of documents becomes a knowledge base you can explore. Fifth: first-pass analysis at scale. I had three language models classify twelve thousand articles on three axes — how they portray Islam, how subjective they are, and how central religion is to the piece. It cost about thirty-five euros and a day of batch processing, against months of manual coding. Again, this is triage, not reading. Two things I'd flag. Where the models disagree is itself a signal worth a look. And a pattern surfaced that I would have missed: surprisingly positive portrayals of Muslim communities inside coverage of Sahelian terrorism. The whole dashboard, again, written without my hand-coding a line.

The stakes

Western tools, non-Western sources

The costs
  • Training data overwhelmingly Western — concepts read through European lenses
  • Labour: Kenyan annotators paid ~$5/day; similar conditions elsewhere
  • Infrastructure: strongest models in US firms; only China credible as an alternative
  • Opaque and non-reproducible: the same input can yield different answers
What it makes possible

Research at scales, and with documents, not previously feasible — degraded scans, under-resourced languages, corpora too large to read.

Held in tension Neither uncritical adoption nor refusal. Use the tools, and disclose what they cost.
I don't want to sell this without the costs. The training data is overwhelmingly Western, so these models read other societies through European categories. The labour behind them is real and badly paid: Kenyan annotators on about five dollars a day, and similar conditions elsewhere. The infrastructure is concentrated — the strongest models sit in a handful of US firms, with only China credible as an alternative. And the systems are opaque and non-reproducible: the same input can give different answers. I hold all of that against the other side of the ledger. AI makes research possible at scales, and with documents, that simply weren't reachable before. The honest position is to use the tools and disclose what they cost.

So where does that leave us. The technology is no longer the obstacle: you don't need a big team or deep coding. And you can now download capable models and run them on a university server, on your own terms — that's moving fast. The division of labour holds: AI organises, we interpret, always. My advice is to start with sources you know well, so you can catch the errors. Because the scans sitting dormant on our hard drives are the raw material of the next phase.
