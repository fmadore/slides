To provide some context for this website, I need to talk briefly about the Africa Multiple Cluster of Excellence. It is a major African studies cluster based at the University of Bayreuth. It has been funded by the German Research Foundation since 2019 and is now in its second phase, which runs until 2032. It comprises over three hundred researchers organised through five research centres in five countries. The overarching theme is an agenda called 'Reconfiguring African Studies': reworking the field in terms of both its concepts and its structures, and confronting the power imbalances in how knowledge about Africa is produced and disseminated. Three concepts developed by the cluster are particularly relevant to this presentation: 'multiplicity', which prioritises relationships over fixed entities; 'relationality', which considers the creation, dissolution and silencing of worlds; and 'reflexivity', which involves examining our own positions and the power behind them.

My team is the Digital Research Environment (DRE). Our role is to make the cluster's research data findable, reusable and presentable. Our work falls into four categories. Firstly, we manage and curate data, including metadata schemas, designing ontologies and taxonomies, and archiving datasets. Secondly, we do data science, including text mining, network analysis and scraping. Thirdly, we are experimenting with using local open-source AI models to perform OCR, transcription, entity extraction and tagging. Fourthly, we focus on dissemination through dashboards, maps, Omeka S databases and websites.

Two principles guide our work. Firstly, description is a joint effort. We don't describe other people's material for them; curation and description are shared with our partner centres. Each centre and its researchers describe the data they know best, and the DRE runs the shared infrastructure that connects these descriptions. Secondly, storage remains distributed by default. We don't transfer all the data to one server in Germany. The data remains in its local repository. Bayreuth holds the metadata layer — the descriptions and links pointing back to it. This is how we attempt to address debates on data colonialism and extraction. Research data becomes findable without being relocated. Now, let me explain how we built that, starting with the system we began with.

The first phase

We began with WissKI

A semantic research-data system built on knowledge graphs — entities and their relations, tied to shared ontologies.

WissKI — Wissenschaftliche Kommunikationsinfrastruktur, "scientific communication infrastructure".

In the first phase, the connecting infrastructure was a system called WissKI. The aim was to enable the cluster's research data to be searched and explored across the centres, allowing you to find collaborators and see what was happening elsewhere. It was built on knowledge graphs, which represent things as entities and the typed relations between them — subject, relation, object — and tie those entities to shared ontologies and open data on the web. Knowledge graphs were a major focus for us in that phase. However, we were limited in various ways and wanted to develop a more user-friendly system for researchers.

Now, how you actually find things across all of it — search.

Facets, filters, autocomplete — built on Typesense

Discovery is the other half of what AMIRA does, and we built it in-house. The search runs on Typesense — a fast, open-source engine — on our own server; the search key never reaches the browser. What it gives you is instant, type-ahead autocomplete and deep faceted filtering: research items filter by year, type, place of origin, country, language, subject and more; people by affiliation and role; projects by funder and section. There are twelve separate corpora — research items, projects, publications, podcasts, videos, people, organisations and the authority terms — and one federated search across all of them, with suggestions grouped by type. The cluster's premise: nothing here is best understood alone.

Of the cluster's three concepts, relationality is the one the atlas is built to show — entities through their ties, not in isolation.

One idea drives the cluster's whole research agenda, and it shapes the atlas. The cluster works with three concepts: multiplicity, relationality, reflexivity. The middle one matters most today. Relationality says that a person, a project, a place, a publication is best understood through its relationships, not on its own — that entities are, in part, the product of the ties they sit in. Now, a catalogue runs on the opposite instinct: one record, one thing, described in isolation, filed away. So the design question for the atlas was easy to state and hard to do. Can the archive show the relationships, and not only the records? The next few slides are what that looks like in practice.

Custom Omeka S modules · entity network

Discursive communities

An entity network of subjects: terms that keep appearing together cluster into communities — themes the collection holds in aggregate, not in any one item.

The catch, already visible These clusters are built from subject keywords. They are only ever as good as that description.
This last view is the one I find most suggestive, and also the most fragile. We take the subject keywords attached to items, and we look at which ones co-occur — which subjects keep turning up together across the collections. Cluster those co-occurrences and you get what we call discursive communities: groupings of themes that no single catalogue record would ever announce on its own. It's a way of asking what a collection is about in aggregate, rather than what any one item happens to be tagged with. And here the catch is already in plain sight. This whole view is built from subject description — from keywords. If the keywords are thin, or skewed, or borrowed from a vocabulary that doesn't fit the material, then the communities it draws are thin and skewed in the same way. So the most ambitious relational view rests on the least reliable part of the metadata. That is the turn in the talk.

An atlas of relations is only as strong as its weakest metadata.

And the weakest, almost always, is subject description — the part researchers supply most sparingly of all.

So here's the honest middle of the talk. Everything I've shown depends on metadata, and metadata is supplied by busy researchers who would much rather be doing their research. Names, dates, places — those come in reasonably well, because they're concrete and you can't write the project up without them. Subject description is the part that gets skipped. It's optional, it takes thought, and it's the first thing to go when a deadline is close. Which is awkward, because subject description is exactly what the relational views lean on hardest. The atlas is most ambitious precisely where its data is thinnest. That isn't a complaint about colleagues; it's a structural fact about how collections actually get made, everywhere. The interesting question is what we do about it — and that splits in two.

When terms do exist

Whose subject headings?

Where subject terms exist, they tend to come from Library of Congress Subject Headings.

  • Our pipeline matches subjects against the LCSH API
  • LCSH was built elsewhere, for other collections
  • African concepts and languages fit it poorly
  • Borrowed terms reshape what can be found
The vocabulary problem Two people pick the same term for the same thing under a fifth of the time (Furnas et al., 1987). One imposed vocabulary makes that worse, not better.

Risam (2018): standard schemas can carry epistemic violence — categories that erase as they describe.

When subject terms do exist, where do they come from? Mostly from Library of Congress Subject Headings — LCSH, the big American controlled vocabulary. Our own pipeline does this automatically: it takes a subject term and reconciles it against the LCSH authority. That's convenient, it's interoperable, and it quietly imports a problem. LCSH was built somewhere else, for other collections, around other assumptions about what matters. African concepts, African-language terms, African knowledge systems often have no good home in it. You either force a near-match or drop the term, and either way the description bends toward what the vocabulary already knows. Two ideas from the literature sharpen this. Furnas and colleagues showed, back in 1987, that two people choose the same word for the same thing less than a fifth of the time; vocabulary is slippery even within one language. And Roopika Risam argues that standard schemas can do a kind of epistemic violence — the category that describes also erases, when it has no room for the thing in front of it. So we carry two problems at once. The description is thin. And where it exists, it's borrowed, which means someone else's categories decide what fits.

Still exploratory

Building the entities the vocabulary lacks

Wikidata, not only Library of Congress

Open, multilingual, editable by communities rather than one national library.

  • Reconcile people, places, institutions to Wikidata
  • Create the African entities it still lacks
AI to help, not to decide

Language models draft candidates at scale; people review and approve.

  • Draft labels, definitions, links for review
  • Curators and centres keep the final say
The honest status Exploratory, by design. The aim is not a finished vocabulary — it is to move authority over description closer to the people the data is about.
So what do we do? Our thinking here is genuinely exploratory, so I'll show a direction, not a solution. The first move is to shift authority work away from Library of Congress and toward Wikidata. Wikidata is open, multilingual, and editable — communities can add and correct entities, instead of waiting on one national library. We already reconcile people, places and institutions against it. The harder and more interesting part is creating the African entities and concepts that Wikidata itself still lacks, so that the right terms exist to be used at all. That's a great deal of careful work, and it's where AI earns a role — a narrow one. Language models are good at drafting: proposing a candidate entity, a label in several languages, a short definition, some links to check. They are not good at deciding what is true. So the model drafts at scale, and curators and the centres review and keep the final say. The goal isn't a perfect vocabulary. It's to move the authority over description closer to the people and places the data is actually about.

Who holds authority over how knowledge is described — and therefore found?

In an era of contested truth, that is what reimagining the archive has to confront.

So I end where the conference begins, but through a side door. The post-truth worry is usually about forgery and denial: false things dressed up to look true. Underneath it sits something quieter and just as decisive, which is description. What gets a name, in whose language, decides what can be found at all. A record that can't be described in terms that fit it isn't argued with or refuted; it simply never surfaces. That's a soft erasure, and it long predates AI — the colonial library did it with catalogue cards. The atlas, with all its graphs and networks, makes the stakes legible, because every relationship it draws runs back through a keyword that someone chose, or didn't. Reimagining the archive, for me, means taking that single choice seriously, and moving the authority over it closer to the people the knowledge belongs to.

To close. The eye-catching part of this project is the visualisations, and they're genuinely useful. But the foundation is the unglamorous part: who describes the material, and in whose words. Description is the first act of the archive — it comes before access, before discovery, before any network graph. Get it wrong and everything downstream inherits the error, very persuasively. So the work I care about most is the quiet work: building the entities that don't yet exist, in vocabularies that fit, with the centres and the communities holding the pen. The atlas is online if you'd like to explore it afterwards, and, as I said at the start, a fair amount of your own institution's research is already in there. Thank you.
