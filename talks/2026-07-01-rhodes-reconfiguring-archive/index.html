To provide some context for this talk, I need to say a word about the Africa Multiple Cluster of Excellence. It is a major African studies cluster based at the University of Bayreuth. It has been funded by the German Research Foundation since 2019 and is now in its second phase, which runs until 2032. It comprises over three hundred researchers organised through five research centres in five countries. The overarching theme is an agenda called 'Reconfiguring African Studies': reworking the field in terms of both its concepts and its structures, and confronting the power imbalances in how knowledge about Africa is produced and disseminated. Three concepts developed by the cluster are particularly relevant to this presentation: 'multiplicity', which prioritises relationships over fixed entities; 'relationality', which considers the creation, dissolution and silencing of worlds; and 'reflexivity', which involves examining our own positions and the power behind them.

My team is the Digital Research Environment (DRE). Our role is to make the cluster's research data findable, reusable and presentable. Our work falls into four categories. Firstly, we manage and curate data, including metadata schemas, designing ontologies and taxonomies, and archiving datasets. Secondly, we do data science, including text mining, network analysis and scraping. Thirdly, we are experimenting with using local open-source AI models to perform OCR, transcription, entity extraction and tagging. Fourthly, we focus on dissemination through dashboards, maps, Omeka S databases and websites.

Two principles guide our work. Firstly, description is a joint effort. We don't describe other people's material for them; curation and description are shared with our partner centres. Each centre and its researchers describe the data they know best, and the DRE runs the shared infrastructure that connects these descriptions. Secondly, storage remains distributed by default. We don't transfer all the data to one server in Germany. The data remains in its local repository. Bayreuth holds the metadata layer — the descriptions and links pointing back to it. This is how we attempt to address debates on data colonialism and extraction. Research data becomes findable without being relocated. Now, let me explain how we built that, starting with the system we began with.

The first phase

We began with WissKI

A semantic research-data system built on knowledge graphs — entities and their relations, tied to shared ontologies.

WissKI — Wissenschaftliche Kommunikationsinfrastruktur, "scientific communication infrastructure".

In the first phase, the connecting infrastructure was a system called WissKI. The aim was to enable the cluster's research data to be searched and explored. This system was built on knowledge graphs, which represent entities and the typed relations between them — subject, relation and object — tying those entities to shared ontologies and open data on the web. However, we were faced with various limitations and wanted to develop a more user-friendly system for researchers.

The DRE has recently launched AMIRA (the Africa Multiple Interactive Research Atlas), which is the cluster's new platform for exploring and visualising its research data, publications, and outputs. Built using Omeka S, it allows you to browse research items, publications, podcasts, YouTube videos, people and organisations, and features search, autocomplete and faceted filters. You can also visualise charts, maps, and network graphs showing who works with whom, where projects are located, and which themes cut across the collection. Everything is cross-linked: click on a person, project, subject or place to land on its detail page with its web of connections.

Facets, filters, autocomplete — built on Typesense

I developed a custom search module using Typesense, a fast open-source engine, on our own server. It provides instant autocomplete and deep faceted filtering. Research items can be filtered by year, type, place of origin, country, language, subject and more, while people can be filtered by affiliation and role, and projects by funder and section.

Here an entity is the sum of its relationships, rather than a standalone record.

Relationality, one of the cluster's three concepts, treats phenomena as formed through their relationships — and attends to the worlds those relationships leave unmade or silenced.

Here, I focus on one of the cluster's three concepts: relationality. It proposes that a person, project or publication is formed through their relationships, and asks what those relationships leave out. So the question is whether the archive can demonstrate these relationships — and Omeka S, the platform AMIRA runs on, turns out to be genuinely helpful for showing those connections.

This is the whole collection seen as a network: entities — people, projects, subjects, places — and the links recorded between them in the knowledge graph. It's the most eye-catching of the views and the most fragile, because every link was described by someone. The network is only ever as good as that description — and the thinnest part of the description, almost always, is subjects. That is the turn I want to make next.

An atlas of relations is only as strong as its weakest metadata.

The weakest part is almost always subject description — the part that researchers supply the least of all.

Everything I've shown relies on metadata, which is supplied by researchers who would rather be conducting research or working on papers. While information such as names, dates and places is provided fairly well, subject descriptions tend to be much less comprehensive. This isn't a criticism of my colleagues; metadata intake is rarely researchers' favourite part of the job.

When terms do exist

Whose subject headings?

Where subject terms exist, they tend to come from Library of Congress Subject Headings.

  • Our pipeline matches subjects against the LCSH API
  • LCSH was built elsewhere, for other collections
  • African concepts and languages fit it poorly
  • Borrowed terms reshape what can be found
The vocabulary problem Two people pick the same term for the same thing under a fifth of the time (Furnas et al., 1987). One imposed vocabulary makes that worse, not better.

Risam (2018): standard schemas can carry epistemic violence — categories that erase as they describe.

When subject terms do exist, where do they come from? Mostly from Library of Congress Subject Headings — LCSH, the big American controlled vocabulary. Our own pipeline does this automatically: it takes a subject term and reconciles it against the LCSH authority. That's convenient, it's interoperable, and it quietly imports a problem. LCSH was built somewhere else, for other collections, around other assumptions about what matters. African concepts, African-language terms, African knowledge systems often have no good home in it. You either force a near-match or drop the term, and either way the description bends toward what the vocabulary already knows. Two ideas from the literature sharpen this. Furnas and colleagues showed, back in 1987, that two people choose the same word for the same thing less than a fifth of the time; vocabulary is slippery even within one language. And Roopika Risam argues that standard schemas can do a kind of epistemic violence — the category that describes also erases, when it has no room for the thing in front of it. So we carry two problems at once. The description is thin. And where it exists, it's borrowed, which means someone else's categories decide what fits.

Still exploratory

Building the entities the vocabulary lacks

Wikidata, not only Library of Congress

Open, multilingual, editable by communities rather than one national library.

  • Reconcile people, places, institutions to Wikidata
  • Create the African entities it still lacks
AI to help, not to decide

Open-source local LLMs draft candidates at scale; people review and approve.

  • Draft labels, definitions, links for review
  • Curators and centres keep the final say
Exploratory by design To move authority over description closer to the people the data is about.
So what do we do? Our thinking here is genuinely exploratory, so I'll show a direction, not a solution. The first move is to shift authority work away from Library of Congress and toward Wikidata. Wikidata is open, multilingual, and editable — communities can add and correct entities, instead of waiting on one national library. We already reconcile people, places and institutions against it. The harder and more interesting part is creating the African entities and concepts that Wikidata itself still lacks, so that the right terms exist to be used at all. That's a great deal of careful work, and it's where AI earns a role — a narrow one. Language models are good at drafting: proposing a candidate entity, a label in several languages, a short definition, some links to check. They are not good at deciding what is true. So the model drafts at scale, and curators and the centres review and keep the final say. The goal isn't a perfect vocabulary. It's to move the authority over description closer to the people and places the data is actually about.

Who has the authority to decide how knowledge is described, and therefore how it is found?

In an era of contested truth, this is the challenge that reimagining the archive must face.

To close, the question I want to leave you with. The post-truth worry is usually about forgery — false things dressed up as true. Underneath it sits something quieter and just as decisive: description. What gets a name, in whose language, decides what can be found at all — a record that can't be described in fitting terms is never refuted, it simply never surfaces. So reimagining the archive, for me, means taking that choice seriously and moving authority over it closer to the people the knowledge belongs to. The atlas is online if you'd like to explore it — and, as I said at the start, a good deal of Rhodes's own research is already in there. Thank you.
