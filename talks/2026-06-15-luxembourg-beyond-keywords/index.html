I'm a historian by training. For fifteen years I've worked on Islam in Francophone West Africa — mainly Benin, Burkina Faso, Côte d'Ivoire and Togo. I do interviews, but I also work heavily with West African newspapers and Islamic publications. I'm a bit of a digital hoarder: besides scanning, I collect a lot online: more than 30,000 items in my Zotero library, about 15,000 still unread. I spent years digitising and cataloguing it, and I wanted it in an open database so others could use these hard-to-find sources. I reached agreements with the copyright holders, and in 2023 I launched the Islam West Africa Collection. It now holds more than 14,700 documents on Muslim public life across six countries — newspaper clippings and Islamic publications, magazines, brochures, tracts — about 96% in French. I built it, and I maintain it alone, which is why I turned to language models: text extraction on degraded scans, entity recognition, sentiment analysis. So far I've used AI to enrich the collection; today is about opening it up.

The "El Hadj" problem

Why keyword search falls short

The faceted search of the IWAC.

Keyword search has built-in problems. I mitigated what I could:

  • Spelling variants → authority files merge 20+ forms
  • Forming a query → autocomplete and facets guide you
  • Polysemy → "hadj" = pilgrimage and the "El Hadj" honorific
The real gap The search box assumes you already know it.
I want the collection open to researchers, but also to the general public — especially Muslims from these countries tracing their communities' history. So I built the best search engine I could: facets, autocomplete, authority files that pull the many spellings of a name together. But some problems with keyword search run deeper than any tool can reach. Take "hadj": it returns two unrelated things at once — articles about the pilgrimage, and the men who carry "El Hadj" as an honorific. No facet untangles that. The interaction model is a barrier too: a keyword box assumes you can already name what you want, then judge a ranked list from an algorithm you never see. I've worked with this corpus since 2015; I know how it's tagged, and new users don't — several have told me they find it intimidating. So I went looking for another way in, and a chatbot was the obvious candidate: more and more people ask in plain language, not in keywords and filters.

Why not just ask AI?

Generic answers, dubious sources

Gemini on Islam in Kpalimé: generic and dubiously sourced. See the chat ↗

  • People now ask AI directly, not a search box
  • Chatbots often invent citations — worse for Africa, with far less training data
  • Web search helps, but the sources are often random
  • The IWAC? Crawled by AI bots, yet barely used and badly cited
More and more people skip the search box and just ask an AI. A bare chatbot is frozen at its training data and cut off from the sources, so it invents plausible-looking citations — worse for Africa, thinly represented to begin with. The commercial chatbots now search the web, which does cut the hallucinations. But the sourcing is erratic: they lean heavily on Wikipedia, which has very little on Islam in West Africa specifically. Now and then one pulls a record from the IWAC, but never systematically. What you get is fluent, confident, and generic. Here's Gemini on Islam in Kpalimé in the nineties: a long, polished answer, sourced to "The Christian Chronicle" and a Turkish foundation. And here's the irony: my collection is crawled constantly by their bots, yet ask it precisely about Kpalimé and it has no real grounding in the archive. It can scrape the collection; it can't use it well.

Model Context Protocol · open standard, Nov 2024

Why a server, not just a chatbot?

An MCP server connects an AI assistant to one outside system. Like an app to your accounts:

Gmail Google Calendar Google Drive Slack GitHub → IWAC
RAG

Retrieval-augmented generation. Proven for GLAM, but it chunks records, and retrieves by a similarity the user never sees.

MCP

Ingests nothing. The data stays put; the server holds the logic; the model just asks and answers. modelcontextprotocol.io

The analogy Like IIIF for images: a shared interface, without handing over the originals. Build the server once — Claude, ChatGPT, or open-source models all connect.
The Model Context Protocol is an open standard for connecting AI assistants to outside systems through defined tools. The easiest way to picture it: if you've ever let Claude or ChatGPT into your Gmail, your calendar, or your Google Drive, you've already used an MCP server — and there are ready-made ones for Slack, GitHub, Notion, hundreds of everyday services. A server simply publishes the queries it's willing to answer, and any compliant assistant can call them. What I've built is the same kind of server, only pointed at a scholarly archive instead of an inbox. The analogy I'd reach for is IIIF: just as IIIF exposes high-resolution images through a shared interface without handing over the originals, MCP exposes search and metadata to an AI without giving up the underlying data. The common alternative is retrieval-augmented generation, RAG — proven for heritage, but it copies the collection into an external store, chunks records so they lose their context, and retrieves through a similarity the user never sees. MCP draws the line differently: nothing is ingested, chunked, or trained on. The server holds the logic; the model only handles the two ends — understanding the question and composing the answer. Build the server once, and Claude, ChatGPT, or an open-source model can all connect.

Agent skills

What is a skill.md?

A skill is a methods handbook for a machine reader — loaded only when a request matches its purpose.

  • Competence by reading, not retraining
  • Versioned, reviewed, refined — a prompt is typed once and lost
  • An open standard across agents — no vendor lock-in, like MCP
  • Born in coding agents — the format fits any domain
my-skill/
├── SKILL.md          # Required: metadata + instructions
├── scripts/          # Optional: executable code
├── references/       # Optional: documentation
├── assets/           # Optional: templates, resources
└── ...               # Any additional files or directories

The standard layout of an agent skill — SKILL.md is the only required file.

So what is a skill? A methods handbook, written in plain language, that the agent reads into context when a request matches its purpose. The model gains a specific competence by reading, not by retraining. The difference from a prompt is durability and accountability: a prompt is typed once and lost, whereas a skill file is versioned, reviewed, published, and refined over time. It turns tacit expertise into an explicit, inspectable artefact: how I search this collection, and which biases I have to disclose. Like MCP, skills are an open standard across agents, so none of this locks you to one vendor. They were born in coding — telling an agent how to run a routine or format output — but the format works just as well for the social sciences: how to behave like a historian, how to read a corpus critically, what to know about a particular collection.

Inside the server · semantic search

Search by meaning, not words

1 · Ask Your question

« Islam et laïcité au Burkina Faso »

Plain language — and any language works.

→
2 · Embed Turned into coordinates

An embedding model (Gemini) maps the text to 768 numbers — one point in a space of meaning.

laïcité → [0.07, -0.12, 0.93, …]

Every article was mapped the same way, in advance.

→
3 · Match Nearest points win

Cosine similarity ranks all 12,000+ articles by how close they sit — closest in meaning, not in wording.

Why it matters Finds the right articles even when they never say « laïcité » — secularism, Franco-Arabic schools, family-code debates. The one tool that runs a model live, just to turn your question into coordinates.
This is the one feature that goes past matching words. Keyword search asks which articles contain a string; semantic search asks which mean the same thing. Three steps. You ask in plain language — for example, Islam et laïcité au Burkina Faso — and in any language, because the embedding model is multilingual. That question becomes an embedding: Gemini, for now, maps it to about 768 numbers, a single point in a space of meaning. Every article was put through the same model in advance, so all twelve thousand already sit as points in that same space. The server then measures how close your question sits to each, and returns the nearest. So laïcité surfaces pieces on secularism, Franco-Arabic schools, family-code reform — about the idea even when the word never appears. One caveat: I said the server runs no AI at query time, and this is the exception, embedding your question once and nothing else. Now, how the skill decides which tool to reach for: that's the method.

The IWAC skill in motion

A five-phase research method

Two depths, chosen up front:

Briefdefault a quick scan and a few close reads
Extended the full five-phase analysis below
  1. Scoping — what does the collection even hold for this question, before any keyword?
  2. Systematic searching — French + transliteration variants (Tabaski = Eid al-Adha); log every search, including null results
  3. Deep reading — full text and sentiment, article by article
  1. Triangulation — cross-check articles, publications, references, index entries
  2. Synthesis — findings with source attribution and confidence grades
The point Twenty-two tools give reach. The "judgement" lives in the skill.
The server has the data, but none of the judgement. I wanted the model to carry my own knowledge of the collection as a historian working with primary sources. Twenty-two tools give reach, not judgement, and that judgement has to live somewhere — it lives in the skill. Before any research, the skill asks how deep to go: a quick Brief pass for an overview, or a full Extended analysis; it defaults to Brief. The Extended path is a five-phase workflow modelled on real archival research. Scoping first: what's even here for this question, before a single keyword. Then systematic searching, in French, every search logged, including the ones that come back empty. Deep reading of the strongest articles. Triangulation across articles, publications, references and index entries. And synthesis: findings with explicit sources and a confidence grade — strong, moderate, or weak.

What a server never could

Discipline, disclosure — and a new role

  • Linguistic discipline — search the French corpus in French, whatever language the question is in
  • Bias disclosure — every synthesis states the skew: francophone tilt, missing Arabisants, thin Niger and Nigeria
  • Evidential discipline — claims tagged primary / secondary / AI-derived; absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence
The role shift Cataloguer, then API designer — now author of research methods a machine can follow. The server took a weekend; the skill is where the expertise lives.
The skill encodes three things no server could. First, linguistic discipline: you can ask in any language — English, German — but the collection is French, so the search has to go out in French. Second, bias disclosure: every synthesis states the skew that scoping turns up — the francophone tilt, thin Niger, no Nigerian press. Third, evidential discipline: every claim tagged primary, secondary, or AI-derived, and absence of evidence is never evidence of absence. That last one matters because these models compress disagreement into the single most central story; the confidence grades and the logged null results are the hedge. The role shift: cataloguer, then API designer, now author of research methods a machine can follow. The server took a weekend with AI's help; the skill is where the expertise lives.

The stakes

Whose infrastructure? African collections and AI extraction

39 of 43 heritage institutions hit by AI-bot traffic spikes (Weinberg, GLAM-E Lab, 2025); Wikimedia: 65% of its costliest traffic is bots. Read it ↗

  • The default is extraction — public institutions and open-access labour subsidising commercial AI
  • MCP, a third path — the collection stays put; the institution decides what the AI sees
Yékú's paradox · 2026 Un-digitised, an African archive is invisible to AI; digitised, it's exposed to scraping — the incomplete corpus becomes the single story, the colonial library rebuilt in the training set. Read the essay ↗
Step back from the tooling: who is this for, and who pays? The default today is extraction. A 2025 GLAM-E Lab survey found 39 of 43 heritage institutions hit by AI-bot traffic spikes that ignore robots.txt; Wikimedia reports 65% of its costliest traffic is bots, not people. I've seen a big spike on the IWAC myself, out of the US and China — thousands a day, a lot for a database on Islam in West Africa. Underfunded institutions and unpaid labour are subsidising commercial AI — digital colonialism in its newest form. The stakes are sharper for African collections. Yékú names the paradox: un-digitised, an African archive is invisible to the models, absent from the training data; digitise it, and you expose it to extractive scraping. The incomplete corpus then risks becoming the definitive account, in one uniform authority. MCP offers a third path: the collection stays put, and the institution decides what the AI can see.

Conclusion

A new way in for a wider public — but no replacement for archival research. It finds what you ask for, not what you weren’t looking for: the unexpected find that slow reading turns up.

A few honest caveats to close. I'm not claiming the MCP server solves the problems I've raised; it raises others. And it's genuinely convincing — knowing this collection, I'm amazed by the answers, which is exactly the risk: that we take them for granted. There's a subtler loss too: in the archive the old way you get serendipity, the thing you weren't looking for, and this method gives you only what you ask for. So it's a promising avenue for public access, not a replacement for archival research. The answers are also only as good as the metadata underneath — the unglamorous work of cleaning, standardising and reconciling spellings is the real foundation. And the question the protocol can't settle: CARE asks what FAIR doesn't — not just who controls the data, but who benefits. A West African collection hosted at a German institute: for whom are we doing this? If you'd like to try it, there's now an AI-access page on the collection's own website with connection instructions for both Claude and ChatGPT — the server already runs as a hosted endpoint on the IWAC's domain. What's next: a small open-source model on that server, answering on the website itself, with no install and no account. Thank you.
